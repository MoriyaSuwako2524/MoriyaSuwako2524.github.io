PHY 162a 1

Physics 162a (QM I)

Problem Set 1

1. Consider the following Lagrangian

$$
L = \frac{1}{2} g_{\alpha \beta}(q) \dot{q}^\alpha \dot{q}^\beta
$$

with $\alpha, \beta = 1, \ldots, d$, where $g_{\alpha \beta}$ is called a metric, and has an inverse $g^{\alpha \beta}$ such that

$$
g^{\alpha \beta} g_{\beta \rho} = \delta^\alpha_\rho .
$$

Here $\delta$ is the Kronecker delta function, and the Einstein summation convention is used.

You may apply the Euler–Lagrange equations as before:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^\alpha} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}^\alpha} = 0
$$

(a)

Write the generalized momenta and the generalized forces.

(b)

Show that the Euler–Lagrange equations are equivalent to the geodesic equation:

$$
\ddot{q}^\alpha + \Gamma^\alpha_{\beta \gamma} \dot{q}^\beta \dot{q}^\gamma = 0
$$

where

$$
\Gamma^\alpha_{\beta \gamma} = \frac{1}{2} g^{\alpha \rho}
\left(
g_{\beta \rho, \gamma} + g_{\gamma \rho, \beta} - g_{\beta \gamma, \rho}
\right),
$$

with

$$
g_{\alpha \beta, \gamma} = \frac{\partial g_{\alpha \beta}}{\partial q^\gamma},
$$

which is known as the Levi–Civita connection for the metric $g$.
Particle dynamics on such “curved” spaces appear both in general relativistic situations and also in cases such as vibrations on a bent sheet or other non-flat geometries.

2. Two coupled particles

(a)
Write the Lagrangian for two nonrelativistic particles with positions $x_1, x_2$ and equal mass $m$, moving in one dimension, connected by a spring whose force on $x_1$ is
$$
F_1 = -k(x_1 - x_2 - \ell),
$$
and whose force on $x_2$ is
$$
F_2 = -k(x_2 - x_1 + \ell),
$$
where $\ell$ is the unstretched length of the spring.
The momentum for particle $i$ is the standard $p_i = m \dot{x}_i$.
You may assume particle 1 is always to the right of particle 2.

(b)
Rewrite $L$ in terms of the center of mass
$$
X = \tfrac{1}{2}(x_1 + x_2)
$$
and the separation
$$
x = x_1 - x_2 .
$$

©
Solve the Euler–Lagrange equations in terms of $X, x$ and show that they are equivalent to those in terms of $x_1, x_2$.

1. The Lagrangian and Geodesic Equation

(1.1) Generalized Momentum and Force

The Lagrangian is given by:
$$
L = \frac{1}{2} g_{\alpha \beta}(q), \dot{q}^\alpha \dot{q}^\beta
$$

The generalized momentum $p_\alpha$ is defined as:
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_\alpha &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^{\alpha}} \
&= \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot q^{\alpha}}
\left(g_{ij}, \dot q^i \dot q^j\right) \
&= \tfrac{1}{2}, g_{ij}!\left(\delta_{\alpha}^{i}\dot q^{j} + \dot q^{i}\delta_{\alpha}^{j}\right) \
&= g_{\alpha\beta}(q), \dot q^{\beta}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The generalized force $F_\alpha$ is:
$$
\begin{aligned}
F_\alpha &= \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^{\alpha}} \
&= \tfrac{1}{2}\frac{\partial g_{ij}(q)}{\partial q^{\alpha}} \dot{q}^i \dot{q}^j
\end{aligned}
$$

Hence, $p_\alpha$ corresponds to the momentum conjugate to $q^\alpha$, while $F_\alpha$ represents the generalized force arising from the variation of the metric tensor $g_{\alpha\beta}(q)$.

(1.2) Derivation of the Geodesic Equation

We start from the Euler–Lagrange equation:
$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^{\alpha}} = \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^\alpha}.
$$

Substituting the expressions derived above:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\tfrac{1}{2}\frac{\partial g_{ij}(q)}{\partial q^{\alpha}}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j
&= \frac{d}{dt}\left(g_{\alpha\beta}(q)\dot q^{\beta}\right) \
\tfrac{1}{2}\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial q^{\alpha}}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j
&= \frac{d g_{\alpha\beta}}{d t}\dot q^{\beta} + g_{\alpha\beta}\ddot q^{\beta} \
\tfrac{1}{2}\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial q^{\beta}}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j
&= \frac{\partial g_{\beta\alpha}}{\partial q^k}\dot q^{k}\dot q^{\alpha} + g_{\beta\alpha}\ddot q^{\alpha}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Rearranging and multiplying by $g^{\alpha\rho}$, we obtain:
$$
\tfrac{1}{2} g^{\alpha\rho}!\left(
\frac{\partial g_{\beta\gamma}}{\partial q^{\rho}}\dot{q}^{\beta}\dot{q}^\gamma
- 2\frac{\partial g_{\gamma \rho}}{\partial q^\beta}\dot q^{\beta}\dot q^{\gamma}
\right)
= \ddot q^{\alpha}.
$$

Simplifying gives the standard geodesic form:
$$
\ddot q^{\alpha} + \tfrac{1}{2} g^{\alpha\rho}!\left(
-\frac{\partial g_{\beta\gamma}}{\partial q^{\rho}}\dot{q}^{\beta}\dot{q}^\gamma
+ \frac{\partial g_{\gamma \rho}}{\partial q^\beta}\dot q^{\beta}\dot q^{\gamma}
+ \frac{\partial g_{\beta \rho}}{\partial q^\gamma}\dot q^{\beta}\dot q^{\gamma}
\right)
= 0,
$$

or equivalently,
$$
\ddot q^{\alpha} + \Gamma^{\alpha}{\beta\gamma} \dot q^{\beta}\dot q^{\gamma} = 0,
$$
where
$$
\Gamma^{\alpha}{\beta\gamma} = \tfrac{1}{2} g^{\alpha\rho}
\left(
\frac{\partial g_{\rho\beta}}{\partial q^{\gamma}} + \frac{\partial g_{\rho\gamma}}{\partial q^{\beta}}- \frac{\partial g_{\beta\gamma}}{\partial q^{\rho}}
\right)
$$

is the Levi–Civita connection.
Thus, the Euler–Lagrange equation for this Lagrangian indeed yields the geodesic equation.
Q.E.D.

2. Two Coupled Particles

(2.1) The Lagrangian in $x_1, x_2$

For two equal-mass particles connected by a spring:

$$
\begin{aligned}
F_1 &= -k(x_1 - x_2 - \ell), \
F_2 &= -k(x_2 - x_1 + \ell),
\end{aligned}
$$

and the potential energy is:
$$
U(x_1, x_2) = \tfrac{1}{2}k(x_1 - x_2 - \ell)^2.
$$

Hence, the Lagrangian is:
$$
L = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot x_1^2 + \dot x_2^2) - \frac{1}{2}k(x_1 - x_2 - \ell)^2.
$$

(2.2) Transformation to Center-of-Mass and Relative Coordinates

Define the center of mass and relative coordinate as:
$$
X = \frac{1}{2}(x_1 + x_2), \qquad x = x_1 - x_2.
$$

Then,
$$
\dot x_1 = \dot X + \frac{1}{2}\dot x, \qquad
\dot x_2 = \dot X - \frac{1}{2}\dot x.
$$

Substituting these into $L$, we obtain:
$$
\begin{aligned}
L &= \frac{1}{2}m!\left[(\dot X + \tfrac{1}{2}\dot x)^2 + (\dot X - \tfrac{1}{2}\dot x)^2\right]
- \frac{1}{2}k(x - \ell)^2 \[4pt]
&= \frac{1}{4}m(4\dot X^2 + \dot x^2) - \frac{1}{2}k(x - \ell)^2.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus, the Lagrangian separates into center-of-mass motion and relative motion parts.

(2.3) Euler–Lagrange Equations and Equivalence

For each particle coordinate $x_i$, the Euler–Lagrange equation is:
$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x_i}.
$$

This gives:
$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = F_i, \qquad
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x_i} = m\ddot x_i.
$$

Hence:
$$
F_i = m\ddot x_i.
$$

Combining the two-particle equations yields:
$$
\begin{aligned}
0 &= m(\ddot x_1 + \ddot x_2), \
-2k(x_1 - x_2 - \ell) &= m(\ddot x_1 - \ddot x_2).
\end{aligned}
$$

Now, in terms of $X$ and $x$:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial X} = 0, \quad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} = -k(x - \ell),
$$

and

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot X} = 2m\ddot X, \quad
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ddot x.
$$

Thus, the corresponding Euler–Lagrange equations are:
$$
\begin{aligned}
-2m\ddot X &= 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \ddot X = 0, \[4pt]
-k(x - \ell) - \tfrac{1}{2}m\ddot x &= 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad m\ddot x = -2k(x - \ell).
\end{aligned}
$$

The first describes free motion of the center of mass,
and the second describes harmonic motion of the relative coordinate.

Q.E.D.
