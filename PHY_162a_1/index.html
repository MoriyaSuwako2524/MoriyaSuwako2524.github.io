Problem Set 1

1. Consider the following Lagrangian

L=12gαβ(q)q˙αq˙β

with α,β=1,…,d , where gαβ is called a metric, and has an inverse gαβ such that

gαβgβρ=δρα.

Here δ is the Kronecker delta function, and the Einstein summation convention is used.

You may apply the Euler–Lagrange equations as before:

∂L∂qα−ddt∂L∂q˙α=0

(a)

Write the generalized momenta and the generalized forces.

(b)

Show that the Euler–Lagrange equations are equivalent to the geodesic equation:

q¨α+Γβγαq˙βq˙γ=0

where

Γβγα=12gαρ(gβρ,γ+gγρ,β−gβγ,ρ),

with

gαβ,γ=∂gαβ∂qγ,

which is known as the Levi–Civita connection for the metric g .
Particle dynamics on such “curved” spaces appear both in general relativistic situations and also in cases such as vibrations on a bent sheet or other non-flat geometries.

2. Two coupled particles

(a)
Write the Lagrangian for two nonrelativistic particles with positions x1,x2 and equal mass m , moving in one dimension, connected by a spring whose force on x1 is

F1=−k(x1−x2−ℓ),

and whose force on x2 is

F2=−k(x2−x1+ℓ),

where ℓ is the unstretched length of the spring.
The momentum for particle i is the standard pi=mx˙i .
You may assume particle 1 is always to the right of particle 2.

(b)
Rewrite L in terms of the center of mass

X=12(x1+x2)

and the separation

x=x1−x2.

Solve the Euler–Lagrange equations in terms of X,x and show that they are equivalent to those in terms of x1,x2 .

1. The Lagrangian and Geodesic Equation

(1.1) Generalized Momentum and Force

The Lagrangian is given by:

L=12gαβ(q)q˙αq˙β

The generalized momentum pα is defined as:

pα=∂L∂q˙α=12∂∂q˙α(gijq˙iq˙j)=12gij(δαiq˙j+q˙iδαj)=gαβ(q)q˙β.

The generalized force Fα is:

Fα=∂L∂qα=12∂gij(q)∂qαq˙iq˙j

Hence, pα corresponds to the momentum conjugate to qα , while Fα represents the generalized force arising from the variation of the metric tensor gαβ(q) .

(1.2) Derivation of the Geodesic Equation

We start from the Euler–Lagrange equation:

∂L∂qα=ddt∂L∂q˙α.

Substituting the expressions derived above:

12∂gij(q)∂qαq˙iq˙j=ddt(gαβ(q)q˙β)12∂gij∂qαq˙iq˙j=dgαβdtq˙β+gαβq¨β12∂gij∂qβq˙iq˙j=∂gβα∂qkq˙kq˙α+gβαq¨α.

Rearranging and multiplying by gαρ , we obtain:

12gαρ(∂gβγ∂qρq˙βq˙γ−2∂gγρ∂qβq˙βq˙γ)=q¨α.

Simplifying gives the standard geodesic form:

q¨α+12gαρ(−∂gβγ∂qρq˙βq˙γ+∂gγρ∂qβq˙βq˙γ+∂gβρ∂qγq˙βq˙γ)=0,

or equivalently,

q¨α+Γβγαq˙βq˙γ=0,

where

Γβγα=12gαρ(∂gρβ∂qγ+∂gργ∂qβ−∂gβγ∂qρ)

is the Levi–Civita connection.
Thus, the Euler–Lagrange equation for this Lagrangian indeed yields the geodesic equation.

2. Two Coupled Particles

(2.1) The Lagrangian in x1,x2

For two equal-mass particles connected by a spring:

F1=−k(x1−x2−ℓ),F2=−k(x2−x1+ℓ),

and the potential energy is:

U(x1,x2)=12k(x1−x2−ℓ)2.

Hence, the Lagrangian is:

L=12m(x˙12+x˙22)−12k(x1−x2−ℓ)2.

(2.2) Transformation to Center-of-Mass and Relative Coordinates

Define the center of mass and relative coordinate as:

X=12(x1+x2),x=x1−x2.

Then,

x˙1=X˙+12x˙,x˙2=X˙−12x˙.

Substituting these into L , we obtain:

L=12m[(X˙+12x˙)2+(X˙−12x˙)2]−12k(x−ℓ)2=14m(4X˙2+x˙2)−12k(x−ℓ)2.

Thus, the Lagrangian separates into center-of-mass motion and relative motion parts.

(2.3) Euler–Lagrange Equations and Equivalence

For each particle coordinate xi , the Euler–Lagrange equation is:

∂L∂xi=ddt∂L∂x˙i.

This gives:

∂L∂xi=Fi,ddt∂L∂x˙i=mx¨i.

Hence:

Fi=mx¨i.

Combining the two-particle equations yields:

0=m(x¨1+x¨2),−2k(x1−x2−ℓ)=m(x¨1−x¨2).

Now, in terms of X and x :

∂L∂X=0,∂L∂x=−k(x−ℓ),

and

ddt∂L∂X˙=2mX¨,ddt∂L∂x˙=12mx¨.

Thus, the corresponding Euler–Lagrange equations are:

−2mX¨=0⇒X¨=0,−k(x−ℓ)−12mx¨=0⇒mx¨=−2k(x−ℓ).

The first describes free motion of the center of mass,
and the second describes harmonic motion of the relative coordinate.
